Cours #1 · Thème 1 — Traditions juridiques comparées

Common Law vs Civil Law — Les grandes traditions juridiques

Le monde est divisé en deux grandes familles juridiques. La Civil Law vient du droit romain et repose sur des codes écrits ; la Common Law est née en Angleterre et repose sur la jurisprudence (les décisions passées des juges font la loi).
🇬🇧 English Part — Study Sheet
Sect. 1 & 2 The Two Great Legal Traditions & Sources of Law
Civil Law — The tradition of codes

Civil Law comes from Roman Law. France exported it to Canada (Quebec) and Louisiana. Law is written in codes (Civil Code, Penal Code…). The legislature (Parliament) is the main creator of law. The judge studies the case beforehand → inquisitorial procedure (the judge asks questions, is active in court). Main divide: public law vs private law.

IllustrationImagine a rulebook for a board game. Before you start, every rule is written down. If you have a question, you look it up in the rulebook (the code).
Common Law — The tradition of judges

Common Law was born in England after William the Conqueror (11th century). Law is largely based on case law = judicial decisions made by judges in the past. Each past decision becomes a precedent. Principle of stare decisis: future judges must follow past decisions on similar cases. The judge has no prior knowledge of the case → adversarial procedure (the lawyers fight it out, the judge watches). Main divide: criminal law vs civil law.

IllustrationInstead of a rulebook, imagine a collection of solved puzzles. When you face a new puzzle, you look at how similar puzzles were solved before.
Four sources of law in Common Law countries
  • Common Law (case law): past judicial decisions = precedents
  • Statutes: written laws voted by Parliament/Congress
  • Regulations: rules made by the executive branch
  • The Constitution: the supreme law, above everything else

In the US, the Supreme Court interprets the Constitution — it can say "this law is unconstitutional" and strike it down. Example: in 1973, the Supreme Court said the Constitution protects privacy → right to abortion (Roe v. Wade). In 2023, the new Supreme Court reversed that.

The jury system

In Common Law countries, serious criminal cases are decided by a jury of citizens (peers). The jury says guilty or not guilty. Then the judge gives the sentence (the punishment).

Sect. 4 & 5 Judicial Power & Key Documents
Judicial activism vs judicial restraint
  • Judicial activism: judges actively interpret laws, sometimes going against legislature or executive (e.g., desegregating schools in the 1950s)
  • Judicial restraint: judges limit themselves to applying the law, letting the legislature decide
Key documents in the Common Law tradition
  • Magna Carta (1215): everyone, including the king, is subject to the law — first foundation of the Rule of Law
  • Habeas Corpus: right to a fair trial — the king cannot throw you in prison without a trial. Juries introduced here as protection against arbitrary justice
  • US Constitution + Bill of Rights: the first Republican constitution, drafted before the French one. Two parts: how the three branches work + the Bill of Rights (first 10 amendments): freedom of speech, religion, press, assembly…
NoteIn England, there is NO written constitution. Everything runs on tradition and precedent. No need to write it down.

Le droit civil : la tradition des codes
  • Droit codifié (Code civil, Code pénal…) · le législateur est la source principale

  • Droit basé sur la jurisprudence (case law) : les décisions passées font la loi
  • Principe du stare decisis : les juges sont liés par les décisions antérieures
  • Procédure contradictoire (adversariale) : les avocats mènent les débats, le juge arbitre
  • Distinction principale : droit pénal vs droit civil
La Constitution américaine

La Cour Suprême américaine interprète la Constitution. Elle peut annuler des lois inconstitutionnelles. Exemple : Roe v. Wade (1973) → droit à la vie privée → droit à l'avortement. En 2023, une nouvelle Cour Suprême conservatrice a renversé cette décision.

Cours #2 · Thème 2 — La liberté religieuse

La liberté religieuse — Deux cas emblématiques

Deux personnes, deux États américains, un même principe constitutionnel : la liberté religieuse. Samantha Elauf l'utilise contre la discrimination. Jack Phillips l'utilise pour justifier la sienne. Même droit, buts opposés.
🇬🇧 English Part — Study Sheet
Sect. 1–2 Legal Foundation: What is Religious Freedom?
International texts
  • UDHR 1948, Art. 18: freedom of thought, conscience and religion — includes the right to change religion and to manifest it in public or in private
  • ECHR 1950, Art. 9: freedom to manifest religion in worship, teaching, practice, and observance — alone or in community, in public or in private
  • First Amendment (US Bill of Rights): Congress cannot establish any religion OR prohibit its free exercise. Same amendment also protects: freedom of speech, press, and assembly
Separation of Church and State: two visions
US versionFrench version (laïcité)
About freedom — maximum religious liberty, minimum state interference. A police officer CAN wear a religious symbolAbout equality — no religion above another, public sphere is neutral. A police officer CANNOT wear a religious symbol
Sect. 3 EEOC v. Abercrombie & Fitch (2015) — Samantha's Case
Facts

Samantha Elauf, 17, applied for a job at Abercrombie & Fitch. She wore a headscarf. The store had a "Look Policy" requiring employees to embody a "Classic East Coast college style." Managers never told her the headscarf violated the policy, but decided not to hire her because of it. She filed a complaint with the EEOC (Equal Employment Opportunity Commission).

Supreme Court Decision: 8 to 1 in favor of Samantha
  • A company can have a Look Policy
  • But a Look Policy cannot ban religious symbols without making exceptions for religious accommodation
  • Even a neutral-looking policy discriminates if it ignores religious exceptions
IllustrationYou can say "No baseball caps in the office." But you can't apply that rule to ban a turban or headscarf without offering a religious exception.
Sect. 4 Masterpiece Cakeshop v. Colorado (2018) — Jack's Case
Facts & Decision

In 2012, gay couple David Mullins and Charlie Craig asked Jack Phillips (Christian fundamentalist) to make a wedding cake. He refused, citing religious beliefs. They filed a complaint with the Colorado Civil Rights Commission.

Jack argued: making a cake is a creative activity = a form of expression (free speech) → being forced violates his religious freedom AND free speech.

The Supreme Court ruled narrowly: in this specific case, the Colorado Civil Rights Commission showed hostility to Jack's religion (procedural problem). So Jack won — but the Supreme Court did NOT say "all bakers can refuse gay customers." It was a case-by-case ruling, not a general license to discriminate.

Sect. 1–2 Le cadre juridique
Les textes fondateurs
  • DUDH 1948, art. 18 : liberté de pensée, conscience et religion — inclut le droit de changer de religion et de la manifester en public ou en privé
  • CEDH 1950, art. 9 : même langage — seul ou en communauté, en public ou en privé
  • 1er Amendement américain : liberté de religion + expression + presse + réunion dans le même amendement — car toutes forment l'expression des Pères Fondateurs

Version américaine : liberté maximale, l'État ne choisit aucune religion. Version française (laïcité) : égalité maximale, neutralité stricte dans l'espace public. → Un policier peut porter un symbole religieux aux USA. En France, non.

Sect. 3–4 Les deux affaires américaines
EEOC v. Abercrombie (2015) — Samantha Elauf

Samantha, 17 ans, postule chez Abercrombie & Fitch. Elle porte un voile. L'entreprise a une Look Policy style "Classic East Coast College". Personne ne lui dit que son voile pose problème lors de l'entretien. Elle est recalée.

Cour Suprême : 8 contre 1 en faveur de Samantha. Une entreprise peut avoir une Look Policy, mais elle ne peut pas interdire les symboles religieux sans prévoir d'exception pour les croyants. Même une politique neutre peut discriminer si elle ignore la religion.

Masterpiece Cakeshop v. Colorado (2018) — Jack Phillips

2012 : un couple gay demande un gâteau de mariage. Jack Phillips refuse pour raisons religieuses. Argument : créer un gâteau = activité créative = liberté d'expression. Forcer = violation du 1er Amendement.

Victoire limitée pour Jack : la Commission du Colorado avait fait preuve d'hostilité envers sa religion → vice de procédure. La Cour Suprême n'a pas dit que tout boulanger peut refuser des clients gay. Décision au cas par cas.

Sect. 5–6 Europe · Transfusions
G4S v. Achbita + Bougnaoui (CJUE, 2017, même jour)
  • Achbita : une entreprise PEUT interdire tous les symboles religieux/politiques si la règle est neutre (toutes convictions), clairement écrite, et motivée par l'image de neutralité
  • Bougnaoui : la préférence d'un client n'est PAS une raison valable pour restreindre la liberté religieuse — seule une politique de neutralité formelle de l'entreprise peut justifier une restriction
Transfusions sanguines et Témoins de Jéhovah
  • Adultes — France (2001) : le médecin PEUT passer outre le refus en urgence pour sauver la vie
  • Adultes — Canada (2016) : le médecin NE PEUT PAS. Deux femmes enceintes sont mortes. Respect absolu de la liberté religieuse
  • Enfants — accord universel (France, UK, USA, Canada) : le médecin PEUT transfuser un enfant dont la vie est en danger, même contre la volonté des parents. L'État a un intérêt supérieur à protéger la vie des enfants
À retenir
  • Même droit (liberté religieuse) utilisé dans des sens opposés : Samantha (victime) vs Jack (auteur de la discrimination)
  • EEOC v. Abercrombie : Look Policy possible mais pas sans exception religieuse — 8-1
  • Masterpiece : victoire étroite de Jack (vice de procédure uniquement) — pas un droit général de refuser
  • CJUE (Europe) : neutralité de l'entreprise = motif suffisant · préférence d'un client = motif insuffisant
  • Transfusions : adultes = divergence France/Canada · enfants = accord universel (intérêt supérieur de l'État)

Précisions finales sur les deux affaires phares, puis les grandes questions : quelles limites sont légitimes ? les lois elles-mêmes peuvent-elles violer la liberté religieuse ? comment la vision européenne diffère-t-elle de la vision américaine ?

Sect. 1 Recap: Final Clarifications
Jack Phillips (Masterpiece) — What the Court actually said
  • NOT a general right for religious people to discriminate against customers
  • Only: the Colorado Civil Rights Commission showed hostility to religion → procedural flaw
  • Cake-making for weddings is a creative activity — a specific, limited circumstance
Important nuanceThe Supreme Court was NOT saying "Jack is right in general." It was saying "the process was unfair to Jack in this case." Two very different things.
Samantha Elauf (EEOC v. Abercrombie) — What the Court actually said
  • The Look Policy was unconstitutional because: it made no exceptions for religious dress · it was not clear during the hiring process
  • But the Court did NOT say: companies cannot have Look Policies · companies must always accommodate any religious request
Sect. 2 Legitimate Limits on Religious Freedom
Health and safety — the universal limit

On both sides of the Atlantic, one reason is accepted everywhere: health and safety. Examples: safety equipment at work overrides religious dress · in surgery, a doctor can ask a religious professional to remove their headscarf for hygienic reasons (this is health, not neutrality).

Sect. 3 Can Laws Violate Religious Freedom? Abortion & Roe v. Wade
The Baptist minister's argument before Roe v. Wade

Before Roe v. Wade (1973), many US states banned abortion based on the idea that life begins at conception — a religious (specifically Catholic) definition. A Baptist minister argued: these laws banning abortion are themselves a violation of religious freedom — because they impose one religion's definition of life onto everyone.

Roe v. Wade (1973) → Dobbs v. Jackson (2023)
  • Roe v. Wade (1973): women have a right to privacy → their body is private → right to choose abortion → states cannot ban it. Always controversial: the Constitution never mentions abortion or privacy in those terms
  • Dobbs v. Jackson (2023): the new (conservative) Supreme Court reversed Roe. The Constitution does not protect abortion. Each state decides. The Court was 6 to 3 — 6 of the 9 justices were Catholic (a statistical outlier in a predominantly Protestant country)
The paradoxThe Court claimed neutrality ("abortion isn't in the Constitution"). But the majority was composed of a minority religion that officially opposes abortion. Is that truly neutral?
Sect. 4 303 Creative (2023) — The Next Step
Facts & Ruling (6 to 3 in her favor)

Lorie Smith, a web designer in Colorado, refused to design websites for same-sex weddings and posted this policy publicly. She argued: designing websites is a creative activity = free speech → Colorado's anti-discrimination law forces her to "speak" against her beliefs → violation of the 1st Amendment.

Justice Neil Gorsuch drew a thin but important line:

  • CANNOT say: "I refuse gay customers" (discrimination based on person's status)
  • CAN say: "I refuse to create gay wedding content" (discrimination based on message)
The domino effect concernIf this is now precedent, could any "creative" business refuse service based on content? Where does "creative" end?
Sect. 5 European/French Contrast: Sieur-Azu v. France (ECHR, 2001)
Key European principle & Application: the pharmacists' case (France, 1995)

Key principle: Religious freedom protects the person's private sphere of conscience — but NOT all public conduct inspired by that conscience.

Application: Two French pharmacists refused to sell contraceptive pills to three women, citing religious beliefs. The court ruled: they were wrong. Pharmacists have a social role in the health sector. When acting as pharmacists, they act on behalf of society — not according to their personal conscience. The law determines what they must do, not their religion.

A Jehovah's Witness doctor must perform blood transfusions. Their job is determined by society's rules, not their private beliefs.

Sect. 3–5 Lois, avortement, 303 Creative, Europe
Avortement : les lois peuvent-elles violer la liberté religieuse ?

Avant Roe v. Wade (1973), les lois américaines interdisant l'avortement étaient fondées sur la définition catholique de la vie (commence à la conception). Un pasteur baptiste a argué : ces lois elles-mêmes violent la liberté religieuse car elles imposent la définition d'une seule religion à tous.

  • Roe v. Wade (1973) : droit à la vie privée → le corps est privé → droit à l'avortement
  • Dobbs v. Jackson (2023) : renversement de Roe · 6 juges sur 9 sont catholiques (minorité statistique aux USA) · chaque État décide désormais
303 Creative (2023) · Pharmaciens français (1995)
  • 303 Creative : Lorie Smith, webdesigner, refuse des sites pour mariages homosexuels. 6-3 en sa faveur. Distinction Gorsuch : refuser des clients gay = interdit · refuser du contenu gay = autorisé
  • Sieur-Azu c. France (CEDH, 2001) : la liberté religieuse protège la sphère privée de conscience — pas tous les actes publics qui en découlent
  • Pharmaciens français (1995) : refus de vendre des pilules contraceptives → condamnés. En tant que pharmaciens, ils agissent au nom de la société, pas selon leur conscience privée
À retenir
  • Limite universelle : santé et sécurité justifient de restreindre la liberté religieuse partout
  • Roe v. Wade (1973) renversé par Dobbs (2023) — 6/9 juges catholiques dans un pays majoritairement protestant
  • 303 Creative : distinction personne (interdit de refuser) vs message (autorisé de refuser)
  • Vision européenne (Sieur-Azu) : liberté religieuse = sphère privée de conscience ≠ actes publics professionnels
  • Pharmaciens : rôle social défini par la loi, pas par la religion personnelle
Cours #4 · Thème 3 — L'Affirmative Action

L'Affirmative Action — Origines et histoire

Comment corriger les effets de 200 ans d'esclavage et 100 ans de ségrégation ? L'Affirmative Action est la réponse américaine : des politiques actives pour rétablir l'égalité dans l'accès à l'emploi et à l'éducation. Mais cette réponse soulève une question insoluble : peut-on corriger une discrimination par une autre ?
🇬🇧 English Part — Study Sheet
Sect. 1–2 University Admissions & Definition
Classic admission tools
  • GPA (grade point average): school grades
  • SAT (Scholastic Assessment Test): standardized test — same for all schools, to level the playing field between good private schools and poor public schools
ProblemAn 80% from a top private school in Cambridge, MA ≠ an 80% from a poor public school in Orlando, FL. The SAT levels the playing field.
Definition & vocabulary

Affirmative action = a policy aimed at correcting the effects of past discrimination in employment or education for members of specific groups (historically: women and people of colour).

⚠️ Vocabulary warningIn the US: affirmative action. In the UK: positive discrimination. Do NOT say "positive discrimination" in an American context.

Two goals: equality of opportunities (equal access) vs equality of outcomes (equal results — more ambitious, more controversial). Illustration: opportunity = everyone gets a ladder. Outcomes = everyone actually reaches the same shelf.

Sect. 4–5 Civil Rights Movement & Busing
Key legislative steps
  • Executive Order 10925 (Kennedy, 1961): the lawyer Harris Wofford Jr. coined the phrase "affirmative action" — meaning active steps to ensure non-discrimination in federal agencies and contracts
  • Civil Rights Act (1964): banned discrimination based on race, color, religion, or national origin in voting, education, and public facilities. Gave the federal government power to enforce desegregation
  • 24th Amendment (1964): abolished poll taxes that prevented poor Black Americans from voting
  • Executive Order 11246 (Johnson, 1965): shift from equality of opportunities to equality of outcomes — "We seek not just equality as a right and a theory, but equality as a fact and as a result"
Busing & Swann v. Charlotte-Mecklenburg (1971)

Transporting Black students from Black neighborhoods to white schools by bus. Swann (1971): the Supreme Court ruled busing is legal if ordered by a state authority. Numbers: 1967: 22% of Black students in the South in integrated schools → 1972: 44% (doubled in 5 years). But busing also had a paradox: as Black students arrived in white schools, white families moved to the suburbs, creating new de facto segregation.

Cours #5 · Thème 3 — L'Affirmative Action

L'Affirmative Action — Des principes aux affaires judiciaires

Post-Brown, la désségrégation avance lentement. Les origines théoriques de l'affirmative action remontent aux syndicats de 1935, bien avant la question raciale. L'affaire Bakke (1978) pose la première grande limite : les quotas raciaux sont inconstitutionnels, mais la race peut rester un facteur parmi d'autres.
🇬🇧 English Part — Study Sheet
Sect. 1 From Brown to Broader Desegregation
Key milestones after Brown
  • Brown II: many southern states refused to comply with Brown → Supreme Court had to affirm that federal court rulings bind all states
  • Loving v. Virginia (1967): anti-miscegenation laws finally struck down — 13 years after Brown, it was still illegal in Virginia for a Black and a white person to marry
  • The judiciary alone was not enough: Kennedy and Johnson used executive orders · federal agents escorted Black children to school · the president sent military troops to a university in Little Rock, Arkansas, where the governor had sent state troops to block Black students
Sect. 2 Theoretical Origins of Affirmative Action
First use of "affirmative action": unions (1935)

The term "affirmative action" first appeared in the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA) of 1935 — to protect unionized workers. If an employer fired someone for joining a union, they had to rehire the person or pay compensation. So: affirmative action was first about unions, not race. The goal was the same: correct an injustice that had already occurred.

New Deal and racial quotas (1930s)

Roosevelt's Public Works Administration (PWA) built roads, bridges, dams. Secretary Harold Ickes implemented racial quotas: a certain percentage of workers had to be Black. This was the first system of racial quotas in US history.

Opportunities vs Outcomes: the evolution
EraGoalLogic
Kennedy / early (1961)Equality of opportunitiesMake sure everyone has a fair shot
Johnson (1965)Equality of outcomes"Not just equality as a right and theory, but equality as a fact and as a result"
The problemTo get more Black students into good schools, you have to reserve seats — which means taking seats from white students. You're helping some by limiting others. Is that fair?
Sect. 4 The First Legal Challenge: Bakke (1978)
Facts: Regents of the University of California v. Bakke

Allan Bakke — a white Californian — applied twice to the medical school of UC Davis. He was rejected both times. Some minority students with lower test scores were admitted because 16% of seats were reserved for minorities (quotas).

Bakke argued: this is reverse discrimination — a violation of the 14th Amendment (Equal Protection Clause) AND the Civil Rights Act of 1964. The irony: the same laws that created affirmative action were being used to challenge it.

Supreme Court ruling: deeply divided (6 different opinions among 9 justices)
  • ✅ Race can be used as one factor among many — diversity is a legitimate goal
  • ❌ Racial quotas are unconstitutional — Bakke was right
The compromise: you can consider race. You just can't have a quota. Think about the individual, not the group.

Cours #6 · Thème 3 — L'Affirmative Action

L'Affirmative Action — Les batailles judiciaires jusqu'à son interdiction

De 2003 à 2023, la Cour Suprême précise, affine, puis finalement abolit l'affirmative action. La distinction clé : les points automatiques par race (Gratz 2003) vs l'examen holiste individuel (Grutter 2003). En 2023, même l'examen holiste est jugé inconstitutionnel.
🇬🇧 English Part — Study Sheet
Sect. 1 University of Michigan Cases (2003)
Gratz v. Bollinger (Undergraduate) — 6 to 3: Gratz WINS

Jennifer Gratz and Patrick Hamacher were rejected. The university used a point system: minority applicants received automatic 20 bonus points because of their race alone (1/5 of the 100 minimum). Ruled: the point system was unconstitutional — giving automatic bonus points to entire racial groups is mechanical, it doesn't consider individuals.

Grutter v. Bollinger (Law School) — 5 to 4: University of Michigan WINS

Barbara Grutter was rejected. Unlike the undergrad system, the law school used a holistic review: examining each application individually, considering all aspects of the person. Race was one factor among many. Ruled: the holistic review is constitutional — race is one factor among many, the goal of a diverse student body is legitimate, individual profiles are considered.

Justice Sandra Day O'Connor: "In order to cultivate a set of leaders with legitimacy in the eyes of the citizenry, it is necessary that the path to leadership be visibly open to talented and qualified individuals of every race." → Society needs Black lawyers and Black judges.

Combined ruling: what is and is not acceptable
  • ✅ A diverse student body is a legitimate educational goal
  • ✅ Race can be a factor in a holistic review
  • ❌ Quotas are unconstitutional
  • ❌ Point systems based on race are unconstitutional
Sect. 2–3 Fisher (2016) & Trump/Sessions
Fisher v. University of Texas (2016) — Last win for affirmative action

The University of Texas had a "Top 10% policy": the top 10% of any Texas high school class automatically got a place. For the remaining places, a holistic review including race was used. Abigail Fisher (white, NOT in the top 10%) argued the holistic review discriminated against her. The Supreme Court upheld the process as constitutional.

This was 2016 — the last year of the Obama administration. Notice the evolution: Johnson (1965) wanted equality of outcomes. Obama (2016) only talks about equality of opportunities. The language has shifted over 50 years.

Trump's first term: dismantling affirmative action
  • Jeff Sessions (AG): universities should NOT consider race as a factor. Quote: "All persons… should be judged by their character, talents and achievements — not the colour of their skin." (echoing MLK's language against civil rights)
  • Yale sued (2020): allegedly discriminating against white and Asian American applicants
  • 3 new conservative justices nominated by Trump → shifted the court's ideological balance
  • Biden (2022): dropped the charges against Yale. But Trump would be back…
Sect. 4–5 Harvard (2023) — The End of Affirmative Action
Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard — 6 to 3 against affirmative action

Led by conservative activist Edward Blum, mostly Asian American students rejected from Harvard. Accusation: Harvard's holistic review was actually racial balancing — engineering the racial composition of its student body. Asian American applicants were rated lower on subjective qualities (likability, social skills) due to racial stereotypes by mostly non-Asian reviewers.

Supreme Court ruling: Affirmative action in university admissions violates the Equal Protection Clause of the 14th Amendment. Race cannot be considered in any admission policy — not in quotas, not in points, not in holistic review. All previous decisions (Bakke, Grutter, Fisher) overturned.

JusticePositionQuote
Chief Justice Roberts (majority)Affirmative action unconstitutionalOverturns all previous decisions
Justice Clarence Thomas (Black, conservative)Against affirmative action"The time has come to end racial preferences in college admissions." Quoted MLK: "All men are created equal."

Real-world impact
  • When California banned affirmative action in 1996: UC Berkeley saw a 60% drop in minority students over 20 years
  • University of Michigan after the ban: Black undergraduates 7% → 4%
  • Public opinion: 63% of US adults believe race should be a factor in college admissions → the Supreme Court ruled against the majority's view
  • Universities can still pursue diversity through socio-economic, geographic criteria, or first-generation college student programs — "You can pursue integration without saying you're integrating."

Cours #7 · Thème 4 — Le droit international des migrations

Migrations — Concepts et cadre juridique international

Avant de comprendre les politiques migratoires, il faut maîtriser les définitions et les textes fondateurs. Qui est "réfugié" ? Qui est "demandeur d'asile" ? La Convention de Genève (1951) pose le cadre. Le système de Dublin organise la réponse européenne — imparfaitement.

Sect. 3 Key International Legal Texts
UDHR (1948): four key rights
  • Right to life → right to flee if your life is in danger
  • Article 13: right to freedom of movement (within your own country; crossing borders is a state's sovereign right)
  • Right to seek asylum: "Everyone has the right to seek and enjoy in other countries asylum from persecution"
  • Article 15: right to a nationality → states cannot leave people stateless
Statelessness examplePalestinians who fled to Syria were given "Palestinian documents" by Syria — not Syrian passports. Their children remained Palestinians. When war broke out in Syria, they had to flee again with documents no country recognized.
The Geneva Convention (1951) — the most important text. Ratified by 196 countries

Definition of a refugee: A person with a well-founded fear of persecution based on: race, religion, nationality, membership of a particular social group, or political opinion — who cannot return to or get protection from their country.

Core principle (non-refoulement): A refugee cannot be returned to a country where their life or freedom is at serious risk.

Who decides if you are a refugee? Nation states, case-by-case. Each country has its own list of safe and unsafe countries of origin.

Sect. 4 The European Framework
The Dublin Convention (1990, effective 1997) — The flawed system

Key rule: The first EU country where an asylum seeker arrives is responsible for processing their application.

The deep unfairness: Italy, Greece, and Spain are at the border — they receive almost all the arrivals and must process all applications. Germany, Sweden, France almost never receive direct arrivals by sea. The system is deeply unfair to border states. This is why Italy and Greece were overwhelmed during the 2015 migrant crisis.

Cours #8 · Thème 4 — Le droit international des migrations

Migrations — Politiques des États-Unis et de l'Australie

Les États-Unis et l'Australie, traditionnellement nations d'immigration, ont radicalement durci leurs politiques. L'Australie invente l'externalisation offshore (Nauru). Trump érige des murs, impose des Travel Bans et veut porter ICE à $85 milliards. Morale récurrente du cours : lutter contre l'immigration coûte extrêmement cher.
🇬🇧 English Part — Study Sheet
Sect. 1 Resettlement
Definition & decline

Resettlement = the transfer of a refugee from the country where they first sought asylum (usually a neighboring country) to a third country that has agreed to permanently host them. Works through bilateral deals. Traditionally the most proactive countries: US, Canada, Australia — all three became much less welcoming. US: from ~70,000/year to ~20,000/year between 2015-2018.

Sect. 2 US Immigration Policy: Trump's First Term
The Border Wall

450 miles of wall, $16 billion. Funded through Pentagon budget → Congress resisted → government shutdown. Trump declared a national emergency (reframing immigration as national security) → Supreme Court agreed: the president can use Pentagon funds. Construction started but never completed during the first term. Biden paused it in 2021 → Trump (second term, 2025) resumed it.

The Travel Ban ("Muslim Ban")

Ban on citizens of 7 predominantly Muslim countries (Syria, Iran, Yemen, Somalia, Libya, Sudan, Chad). Challenged in court as unconstitutional (equal treatment, 1st Amendment). Supreme Court ruling: the ban is legal. Security concerns (terrorism) justify the restriction, even if it infringes on fundamental rights. Later expanded to non-Muslim countries (Nigeria, Eritrea…) to protect the policy from "Muslim ban" accusations.

NoteThe Supreme Court applies US Constitutional law — NOT international law. The Constitution is above international treaties in US jurisprudence.
The Remain in Mexico Policy (2019) & Biden's evolution

Asylum seekers had to wait in Mexico while their US asylum application was processed — 60,000 people stuck in Mexico for up to 18 months. Supreme Court: legal (March 2020). Biden ended it (March 2021) → by 2023, introduced new restrictions resembling Trump's (apply from country of origin or first country traveled through).

The pattern: the US and most Western countries end up in a similar place regardless of the administration. The pressure to restrict is structural.
Sect. 3 ICE and Trump's Second Term
What is ICE?

ICE = Immigration and Customs Enforcement — part of the Department of Homeland Security. NOT the police (FBI). NOT the military. Primarily a bureaucratic administration dealing with paperwork. Irregular migration (overstaying a visa) = a civil violation, not a criminal offense in the US.

Sect. 4 Australia: Offshore Processing
The Nauru solution (2012)

Australia made a deal with Nauru (21 km², 3,000 km northeast of Australia): asylum seekers intercepted before reaching Australian shores were sent to Nauru for processing. Asylum seekers never set foot on Australian soil → far from media and public scrutiny.

Controversial: arbitrary detention (before status determined) · inhumane conditions · possible violation of non-refoulement.

The cost — the course's key moral

Australia paid $284 million per year to Nauru to process 1,500 migrants. Simple math: $284M ÷ 1,500 = ~$189,000 per migrant.

Politicians who say they will "save money" by reducing immigration rarely account for the massive infrastructure costs of enforcement. Anti-immigration policy is extremely expensive.

By 2016, children were included in the offshore facilities. Some children attempted suicide. International pressure forced Australia to resettle children back. By 2019, Nauru was empty — but overcrowding simply shifted to other detention centers.

Cours #9 · Thème 4 — Le droit international des migrations

Migrations — Politiques migratoires européennes et pratiques extralégales

L'Europe répond à la crise migratoire de 2015 par des accords controversés (Turquie, Libye), des pratiques extralégales (refoulements, fermeture de ports) et une désunion politique permanente. Le système Dublin, conçu pour l'ordre, est profondément injuste pour les pays frontaliers. Pendant ce temps, des milliers de personnes meurent en Méditerranée.
🇬🇧 English Part — Study Sheet
Sect. 1–2 Australia's PR Campaign & Dublin's Flaws
The "No Way" campaign (2012) — Australia, first mover

Australia was the first country to develop an aggressive PR campaign to discourage migrants in their country of origin. Slogan: "No Way. You Will Not Make Australia Home." Goal: deterrence. Europe followed with campaigns in Africa emphasizing how difficult life is for migrants there. Counter-argument from migration studies: people don't migrate because they think Europe is paradise — they migrate because something has gone badly wrong at home.

Dublin's deep unfairness

Italy, Greece, Spain = entry doors → must process nearly all applications. Germany, Sweden, Belgium = interior countries → almost never get direct arrivals. The system was not designed for mass migration. The 2015 crisis exposed its limits completely. Dublin II, Dublin III were attempts to fix it. Now we're in a "New Pact on Migration and Asylum" (2024) — still not fully working.

Sect. 4–5 2015 Crisis, EU-Turkey Deal & Italy-Libya Deal
The 2015 crisis and its political consequences

Massive inflow 2015-2018 from Syria, Iraq, Afghanistan. Italy and Greece overwhelmed. Political consequence: this is when anti-immigration politics became mainstream in Europe. You cannot understand Brexit without understanding the 2015 migration crisis.

Germany (Merkel) accepted 1 million Syrian refugees in 2015 — generous but unilateral. Huge backlash in Germany. Germany later joined the restrictionist camp.

€6 billion to Turkey in exchange for: closing borders to migrants heading to the EU · opening detention centers · an exchange (1 irregular migrant returned to Turkey = 1 legitimate Syrian refugee sent to EU). 2020: Erdogan, tired of the deal, partially opened the border. Greece deployed military ships.

The precedent: this deal turned migrants into bargaining chips in international diplomacy.
Italy-Libya Deal (2017) — Extralegal practices

Italy funded and armed the Libyan Coast Guard to intercept migrants before they reach Italian waters. Problem: Libya in 2017 was in civil war. The "Coast Guard" was a faction in that civil war. Slave trade had restarted in Libya. Libya cannot grant asylum to anyone. This violates non-refoulement. But nobody in Europe objected because nobody wanted to host those migrants. Amnesty International has extensively documented the atrocities in Libyan detention centers.

Sect. 6 NGO Rescue Boats and European Responses
Legal obligation to rescue & Salvini's response

Under international maritime law: any captain (commercial, military, NGO, tourist) is legally obligated to rescue people in distress at sea. NGOs (MSF, etc.) deployed rescue boats because Frontex and EU Coast Guards received instructions not to rescue all boats. Human traffickers put 100+ people on small inflatable boats with minimal fuel — without rescue, they die of thirst, starvation, or drowning.

Matteo Salvini (Italian Interior Minister): began refusing to let NGO rescue boats dock in Italian ports — a violation of maritime rescue law. One Italian Coast Guard ship was prevented by Salvini from disembarking its own rescued passengers. An Italian prosecutor sued Salvini for kidnapping and illegal detention.

The numbers

2018: 21,000 people crossed the Mediterranean on small boats. Nearly 10% died (~2,054 confirmed deaths). 2023: A boat carrying 150+ people from Afghanistan, Pakistan, and Somalia crashed off the Italian coast — half died.

Cours #10 · Thème 4 — Le droit international des migrations

Migrations — Front anti-immigration européen et Pacte sur la Migration

L'Europe se fragmente. Un front anti-immigration (Visegrád, Italie, Hongrie, Autriche) s'oppose à un camp officiellement "pro-migrants" (Macron) qui pratique en réalité le refoulement. Le camp de Moria brûle. L'exception ukrainienne révèle les contradictions. Le Pacte de 2024 tente de réformer Dublin. Et Elie Wiesel rappelle : "Aucun être humain n'est illégal."

Sect. 1–3 The Anti-Immigration Front & France's Contradictions
The Visegrád Group (V4) and the Morocco Deal

Poland, Hungary, Slovakia, Czech Republic — historically conservative on migration, formed a political bloc within the EU. Joined by Austria and eventually Germany (after Merkel's CDU shifted right). In February 2019, V4 + Germany + Austria signed a joint deal with Morocco: money and equipment in exchange for reinforced borders and detention centers. Italy's reaction: "See? We're not the only ones doing it."

The Italy-Hungary Alliance (2018)

Salvini + Orbán: shared vision of a "non-welcoming Europe." They targeted Macron as their main opponent. They claimed Macron and George Soros were behind "pro-migrant" policies. Note: the "Soros conspiracy theory" — Soros funds various NGOs but the conspiracy is politically convenient and factually unfounded. In Hungary, it became the central scapegoat for Orbán's anti-immigration narrative.

France: rhetoric vs practice

Macron was seen as "pro-migrant." In reality, France: rejected 2/3 of asylum applications · practiced pushbacks at the border · refused to let the Aquarius (rescue boat with 230 migrants) dock in a French port. When Italy finally allowed the Aquarius to dock in Toulon, France was furious and suspended the relocation of 3,500 refugees from Italy that had been previously agreed upon.

Key observation: migrants became diplomatic leverage — a bargaining chip in EU relations.
Sect. 4–5 Moria Camp & Brexit
Moria Camp (Lesbos, Greece)

Originally designed for 2,200 people. At its peak, held 20,000 people (nearly 10 times capacity). By September 2020: still ~12,000 people. In 2020, the camp burned down in an accidental fire. 12,000 migrants left homeless. With no European solidarity mechanism to relocate them quickly, they were moved to another overcrowded Greek camp.

Brexit and the Illegal Migration Act (July 2023)

Key promise of Brexit: leave the EU = no more obligation to accept EU migration quotas. Illegal Migration Act (July 2023): "No asylum seeker will be allowed to apply for asylum in the UK if they crossed the Channel illegally." The EU Home Affairs Commissioner called it a breach of the Refugee Convention. The UK washes its hands.

The exception: Ukrainians

Ukrainian refugees received preferential treatment under the EU's Temporary Protection Directive: automatic status, right to work, healthcare, education immediately — without lengthy asylum process. Why Ukrainians and not Syrians, Afghans, Somalis? All are fleeing war. The answer is largely racial and geopolitical. By 2025: Poland and Hungary hosting significant numbers wanted them to go back. Protection extended to April 2026 → ongoing political tensions.

Sect. 6–7 EU Migration Pact (2024) & Marrakech (2018)
EU Migration Pact (April 2024)

Voted by the European Parliament to replace Dublin. Key goals: asylum applications processed within 12 weeks · negative answers → immediate return · end administrative limbo.

Mandatory solidarity ("flexible solidarity"): Member states can choose to relocate asylum seekers from Italy/Greece/Spain to their country · pay money · provide operational support · help fund deportations. In practice: nobody wants to take migrants, nobody wants to pay. The "flexible solidarity" framework is a negotiating tool — not a binding, operational policy.

The Marrakech Global Compact (2018)

Signed by 152 countries, rejected by the US (and others). Non-binding — a political declaration, not hard law. Key contributions:

  • Reminds signatories of non-discrimination and non-refoulement
  • Collective expulsion is prohibited: you cannot deport a group without examining individual cases
  • Formal recognition of climate refugees as a category

The philosophical conclusion

Elie Wiesel (Nobel Prize): "No human being is illegal."

International law was built in 1948-1951 to protect as many vulnerable people as possible. Today, Western countries have transformed it into a system of classification — finding ways to deny protection rather than grant it. The priority has shifted: not "how can we protect people" but "how can we avoid taking responsibility."
